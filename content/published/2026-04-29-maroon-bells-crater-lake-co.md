## What 60/100 Means at Maroon Bells in Late April

Sixty is a "yes, but pack a layer" day. The trail is open, the road is open (since we're outside the May–October shuttle window, you can self-drive to the lot), and the air quality is clean — AQI 26, which is the kind of number you usually only see in places that don't have a postcard mountain view to ruin it.

What's keeping the score from breaking 70 is the temperature. The system has us at 13°F at trail elevation right now, with a feels-like of 2°F when the wind kicks. It'll climb to 25°F by midday and 26°F tomorrow before a wet front pushes through Thursday. So the window is narrow but real: today, a couple of hours after the sun gets above the ridgeline, the trail is exactly what people imagine when they imagine the Maroon Bells — Maroon Lake reflecting the two famous peaks, Crater Lake an easy mile and a half farther back, dogs willing.

- **Today (Apr 29):** High 25°F, low 12°F, 24% rain chance, UV 8. Score 60.
- **Tomorrow (Apr 30):** High 26°F, low 11°F, 89% precipitation chance — that's the wet front. Score 60 but I'd give it a real Caution badge if I were sending the alert myself.

- **May 2:** High 36°F, low 13°F, 33% precip, UV 9. **Score jumps to 70.** This is the day I'd target if you can wait three days.

## Kelly and Mark's Assessment

Kelly and Mark are shih tzus with about as much body mass as a loaf of bread between the two of them. They have opinions about cold mornings, and those opinions are: no.

But Maroon Bells Crater Lake is one of the few high-altitude trails I'd take them on without a long internal debate. Here's why.

The trail to Maroon Lake from the welcome center is paved and gentle — half a mile to the lake overlook, the kind of distance Kelly tolerates. Crater Lake adds another 1.4 miles of dirt with about 500 feet of gain, very doable in the easy out-and-back style. There are no creek crossings to negotiate. There are no ledges where a small dog can get into trouble. There are no exposed ridge sections where a wind gust takes them off their feet.

What there is: a parking lot at 9,580 feet, a leashed-dog policy that the rangers actually enforce on weekends, and a marmot population that will absolutely fight a shih tzu if the shih tzu is dumb enough to get within striking distance of a marmot. Both of mine are dumb enough. So we'll be on a six-foot leash, wearing the orange thermal vests, and we'll be turning around at Crater Lake, not pushing for the buckskin and back-country mileage that some of you are tempted to add when the easy trail goes by too fast.

The temperature math for them: at 13°F with a 2°F feels-like, we are not standing around taking pictures for ten minutes. We are walking, we are looking, we are walking back. If you're bringing dogs, particularly small dogs, that's the protocol today. Movement keeps them warm. Standing still does not.

## Why Maroon Bells Specifically, And Why Now

Most CO high-alpine trails are buried under snow until June. Maroon Lake is not. Its elevation (9,580 ft) is high enough to feel like the mountains and low enough to be shoulder-season accessible. The road from Aspen — Maroon Creek Road — is plowed through April, and the famous shuttle restriction doesn't kick in until May 5. So this stretch from late April through early May is the only time of the year when you can drive your own car to the Maroon Lake parking lot during daylight hours.

That's the window. After May 5, you're parking at Aspen Highlands Village and taking the Maroon Bells shuttle, which runs roughly 5am to 5pm and costs around $16 per adult. Before late April, the road is dicey. Right now, you can drive your own car to the lot, walk your own dogs at your own pace, and leave when you want. That flexibility is worth a lot if you have a weather window that doesn't match the shuttle schedule.

The trail itself is a 3.6-mile out-and-back from the welcome center to Crater Lake, with about 500 feet of total gain. Easy by Colorado standards. The grade is gentle the whole way. The footing in late April will be a mix of dry trail at lower stretches and patchy snow at the upper Crater Lake basin — nothing requiring traction devices today, but I'd throw microspikes in the pack if I had them, just in case the morning cold hardened any wet spots into ice.

## What to Bring

For the human:

- Insulation layer (system-flagged required — this is real)
- Wind shell, even though winds are only 10 mph with gusts to 14
- Sunglasses and SPF — UV index 8 above 9,000 ft burns faces in two hours flat
- Microspikes or low-profile traction (optional but easy insurance)
- Two liters of water and something with calories
- Cash or card for the parking fee at the welcome center

For the dog:

- Six-foot leash (no flexi leashes — leashed-dog rules require a fixed-length leash)
- Thermal vest if you have one for a small dog. For a Lab or larger short-coated dog, the standard thinking applies: assess at the parking lot.
- Booties if your dog is sensitive to cold ground. Mark wears them. Kelly will not tolerate them. We've made our peace with this.
- Water bowl and at least 12 oz of water per dog
- Poop bags. There are trash receptacles at the welcome center but not on the trail.

## When Not to Go

Skip this trail today if:

- You're driving from Denver or further. The 3.5–4 hour drive each way for a 3.6-mile trail is not the math, especially with the better Saturday window approaching.
- Your dog is small and very sensitive to cold. Kelly is borderline. If yours is any smaller or thinner-coated, this is a "wait for May 2" call.
- You have any altitude sensitivity. 9,580 ft is real altitude, especially on a cold morning. Hydrate the day before.
- The wet front is your weather window. Friday April 30 is going to be miserable. Don't try to thread it.

Skip this trail in general if:

- You don't like crowds. May–October is shuttle-and-elbows. The whole point of this April–early-May window is the relative quiet.
- You can't keep your dog on a six-foot leash. The rangers will turn you around. They've heard the explanations. They don't care.
