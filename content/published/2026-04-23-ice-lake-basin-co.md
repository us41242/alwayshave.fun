## 🐕 DOG-FRIENDLY (IN SEASON) — Ice Lake Basin, San Juan National Forest, Silverton CO

**Score: 22/100 — Poor conditions**

Upper trail under deep snowpack. Road to trailhead may require high-clearance. No thunder risk — it's still April — but the full trail is effectively inaccessible. Kelly and Mark are not going above the trailhead today.

---

## What 22/100 Means at Ice Lake Basin in Late April

The 22 is not a close call. It's a clean no for anyone trying to hike the full 7 miles and 2,600 feet of gain to the basin. Here's why the number is what it is:

Ice Lake Basin tops out at 12,257 feet. The trail gains almost all of that elevation through the San Juan National Forest on Colorado's southwestern slope — Silverton country, where the snowpack is some of the deepest and most persistent in the state. The April 6 report on this trail gave it a 60. That felt generous even then. Three weeks later, what's changed is the temperature at lower elevations, not the snowpack above 10,000 feet.

At elevation, it's still winter.

The lower sections of the trail are passable in late April — from the South Mineral Campground trailhead up through the first mile or so, you can hike on bare dirt and scattered snow. That's it. Above that, you're on continuous consolidated snowpack, and above 11,000 feet you're punching through unconsolidated crud that's softened during afternoon sun cycles and re-frozen overnight. It's the worst of both worlds: too soft to trust crampons on, too hard to walk without them.

The road itself — the spur to South Mineral Campground off CO-110 — is worth a phone call to the Silverton district office before you drive out. High-clearance is listed as recommended in the seed data, and in late April, you may encounter snow, mud, or both on the approach depending on recent weather cycles. Showing up in a low-clearance vehicle and discovering you can't reach the trailhead is a particularly bad version of this trip.

## Kelly and Mark's Assessment

Kelly and Mark are shih tzus. They've done this trail before — in July, when it looks like a completely different place. In July, the basin is wildflowers and cold clear water and a 12,000-foot view that earns the 7 miles. That's the version they know and like.

The April 23 version is not for them.

I did a drive-out on the Silverton side this week, mostly just to see what the road looked like and to let them stretch on the lower section. They did about half a mile from the trailhead, sniffed a lot of frozen dirt, and looked back at me with expressions that communicated they had opinions. We turned around. That was correct.

If you're bringing dogs to Ice Lake Basin: the trail is dog-friendly by policy (San Juan National Forest, leash required, leave no trace, pack out waste). In season — July through early September — it's a genuinely good dog trail for dogs that can handle sustained elevation gain. Right now, the cold, the snow depth, and the post-holing risk make it a no. Short-legged dogs especially will struggle to maintain body temperature and footing on wet, refrozen snow.

## When to Actually Come

**Late June to early July** is the realistic open window.

The San Juans typically see trail access at this elevation after significant snowmelt, which requires sustained warm days at altitude. The wildflower peak — which is genuinely worth the trip — usually runs from mid-July through early August. If you're planning a first visit, mid-July is the safest bet: road reliable, trail clear, wildflowers starting, thunderstorm window manageable if you're on the summit by noon and descending by 1pm.

The "start before 7am" rule applies hard in summer. The San Juans build afternoon thunderstorms fast and you do not want to be above treeline at Ice Lake when they hit. This is not an exaggeration. Come back in July, start early, and you'll have one of the better days Colorado offers.

## What to Do Instead Right Now (Late April CO)

If you're near Silverton or Durango this weekend and want to be outside, here are lower-elevation options that are actually accessible:

**Cascade Creek Trail (near Durango):** Lower elevation, mostly clear in late April. Dogs allowed on leash. About 3.5 miles one-way following the creek drainage. Significantly easier than Ice Lake Basin and not snowbound at this time of year.

**Animas River Trail (Durango):** Paved, accessible year-round, river views. Good for dogs. Not the backcountry experience Ice Lake is, but if Kelly and Mark need a run, the Animas trail delivers without the risk of ice at altitude.

**Lime Creek Road area:** If you have high-clearance and want to scout the general San Juans corridor, Lime Creek provides valley-floor access to the mountain environment without the commitment to a high-alpine destination. Bring the camera.

## If You Go Anyway

People do go to Ice Lake Basin in late April. Some of them have the kit for it: mountaineering boots, crampons, an ice axe for the steeper sections near the basin, layering systems that work at altitude in cold wet conditions. If that's you, this is a challenging but worthwhile objective.

If that's not you — if you're planning a "hike" in trail runners with a day pack — this is the wrong weekend and the wrong trail. The difference between a 22/100 day at Ice Lake Basin and an emergency is equipment and experience, not willingness.

Come back in July. Bookmark this page. The basin in wildflower season will make you forget this report existed.
