Okay, 60 out of 100 isn't a perfect score, but for Maroon Bells Crater Lake right now, it's a solid 'go, but be smart about it.' You're looking at 30°F, a gentle 8 mph wind, and an AQI of 38 (that's clear enough to count every pine needle on the ridge, by the way). Zero rain chance. The real story here is the cold, and if you’re prepared for it, you’ll have a fantastic day.

Thirty degrees means you're not going to be cruising in a t-shirt. This isn't mid-July, and the trail will remind you of that with every step. The 3.6 miles and 500 feet of gain for Crater Lake sounds like a walk in the park, and technically, it is. But "easy" in early spring or late fall when it's this cold and there's a good chance of lingering ice patches in shaded areas? That's a different kind of easy. It's not a death march, but you'll feel it if you're not dressed right.

Kelly and I took Mark and Hank out here last year in similar temps, and while Hank (the tougher of the two shih tzus, don't tell Mark) was fine, Mark started lifting paws after about a mile. So, if your four-legged hiking buddy isn't built for cold paws, think about some booties. A coat for them isn't a bad idea either. They don't have the same internal thermostat we do, and that snow and ice can be brutal on their pads and joints. You might be tough, but your dog might not be. This is a dog-friendly trail, but that doesn't mean it's a dog-comfortable trail without a little planning.

Now, the biggest hurdle for Maroon Bells Crater Lake, and honestly, any of the Maroon Bells trails, isn't the elevation or the distance. It's the shuttle. From 5 AM to 5 PM, you cannot drive your own private vehicle to the trailhead. Period. You park in Aspen and take the bus. If you show up at 10 AM expecting to roll up to the lot, you're going to be disappointed, angry, and stuck. This is non-negotiable. Plan your shuttle tickets well in advance, especially on weekends, as they do sell out. We've seen more people get turned around at the gate because of this than any other single reason for Maroon Bells hiking. If you're planning an early start to beat the initial chill and the eventual crowds, make sure your shuttle ticket aligns. Or, if you want to avoid the shuttle altogether, you'll need to be through the gate before 5 AM or after 5 PM. That means a very early morning or a late afternoon hike – which, at 30°F, means carrying headlamps and being prepared for even colder temperatures.

So, what does 'use caution' mean when the forecast is clear but cold? It means layers. Base layer, fleece, wind shell. Gloves. A warm hat. You will want them. You might start off feeling cold, but you'll warm up as you move. Then, when you stop for that inevitably perfect photo op, you'll need those layers again to avoid getting chilled. Good, waterproof hiking boots are a must, and if you've got microspikes or something similar, throw them in your pack. There will be icy spots, especially in the shaded parts of the trail that don't see much sun. A thermos of hot tea or coffee will feel like a luxury, not a burden, when you're sitting by Crater Lake. This isn't a summer stroll; it's a proper cold-weather hike, even if it's 'easy' on paper.

Look, Maroon Bells Crater Lake is one of those Colorado trails everyone talks about. Most people see it in peak summer, elbow-to-elbow with hundreds of others, often annoyed by the logistics. Going now, when it's 60/100, means you get a completely different experience: solitude, crisp air, and a stark beauty that's often lost in the summer haze. The conditions are honest: it's cold. But if you pack smart, layer up, and get your Maroon Bells shuttle logistics dialed, you're going to have an incredible hike. This is your chance to see Maroon Bells without the crowds, before the summer rush truly hits the White River National Forest. Don't let a little cold keep you from a truly memorable trip. Go.
