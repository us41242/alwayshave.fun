## The Flash-Flood Read (The Only Thing That Matters)

This is the part of the post that exists because three people have died in Buckskin Gulch in the last decade and I want to be very clear about how to not be the fourth.

**Today's read: safe.**

National Weather Service Flagstaff has zero precipitation in the forecast through Tuesday May 5 for the entire Paria/Vermilion drainage. The 24-hour upstream radar (Kanab, UT and Page, AZ) shows clear skies. There's no monsoon system in the long-range forecast for the next 10 days.

But the rule for this trail isn't "is it raining now." The rule is "is rain in the forecast anywhere upstream within 24 hours of when you'll be in the slot." The Paria/Buckskin drainage system catches rain from a watershed roughly the size of Rhode Island, and a thunderstorm 60 miles north in southern Utah can send a flash flood through Buckskin in 90 minutes with no visual warning at the slot.

**Before you go, check:**
- [NWS Flagstaff forecast](https://www.weather.gov/fgz/) for Coconino + Kane counties (UT)
- [BLM Vermilion Cliffs alert page](https://www.blm.gov/visit/vermilion-cliffs-national-monument)
- The condition kiosk at the Wire Pass trailhead (BLM updates it)

**Cancel if:**
- Any precipitation chance >20% in the 24-hour upstream forecast
- Any thunderstorm watch for southern Utah
- Visible monsoon clouds anywhere to the north

The slot is narrow enough at multiple choke points that there is no escape route once water arrives. Sky-blue right now; check again at 5am the day you go.

## How to Run This Trail With a Dog

**The actual route.** You park at Wire Pass Trailhead off House Rock Valley Road (28 miles south of US-89 between Kanab and Page; it's signed, you'll see the BLM kiosk). The trail starts as a flat sand wash. After about 0.7 miles you enter the Wire Pass slot — a quarter-mile of narrow walls where you can touch both sides at the same time. Wire Pass empties into Buckskin Gulch at the 1.7-mile mark. From there you can walk Buckskin north (toward the Paria) for as long as your legs hold up. Most day hikers turn around at 3.5–4 miles in.

**Dog logistics that matter:**

1. **Leash on, the whole time.** Buckskin is a flash-flood corridor and the BLM is consistent about leash compliance. Riley is good off-leash on most trails, but here we use a 6-foot lead start to finish.

2. **Bring more water than you think you need for the dog.** I bring 1.5L of dog water for Riley plus 3L for myself for a half-day hike. The slot stays cool but the open sand wash before and after is exposed. Riley drinks more than she eats out here.

3. **Pad-check at the turnaround.** Sandstone is hard on dog pads, and Wire Pass has some surface drag. I check Riley's pads at the turnaround and again at the truck. If you see any bleeding or hot spots, the booties go on.

4. **No sniffing into the side cracks.** Buckskin has rattlesnakes and the side cracks in the sandstone are the kind of cool-shaded spaces where they hang out on warm afternoons. Riley has been trained off snake-shaped objects, but I still keep her clear of the cracks. Your dog probably hasn't been trained for this.

5. **No off-trail.** The desert floor around Vermilion Cliffs is biological soil crust — fragile cyanobacteria mats that take 50+ years to recover from a footstep. Stay in the wash.

**For your dog:**

- 6-foot leash (BLM requires)
- Booties if it's >78°F at the trailhead

- Tripods or fragile gear (the slot has tight squeezes)
- Anything you'd be sad to scratch (rolling-pack-on-rock contact is constant)
- A dog that's never been on a 6+ mile hike (this isn't the trail to test it)

## The 90-Second Geology Brief You Didn't Ask For

Buckskin Gulch is one of the longest slot canyons in the world — about 13 miles end to end if you do the full traverse. Wire Pass is a small tributary that joins Buckskin at the 1.7-mile mark. The entire system was carved into Navajo Sandstone over the last million years by water pulling sand grains off the walls one at a time. The deepest sections of Buckskin are 200+ feet below the desert surface and only 5–6 feet wide. You can stand in a place where the sky is a blue ribbon overhead and the walls are close enough that two people can't pass without one of you turning sideways.

Riley does not care about any of this. Riley cares about the smell of the water seeps and whether there's a stick to carry. Both of these are abundant in Wire Pass on a clean weather day.

## When NOT to Go

- **June through early September.** Monsoon season + extreme heat. Even on a clear morning, afternoon storms 50 miles north can flush the canyon. Daytime temps at the trailhead routinely hit 100°F+. Dog paws cannot tolerate the surface temperatures. The flash-flood deaths in this drainage almost all happen during monsoon season. Just don't.
- **December through February.** The slot itself is fine, but the access road (House Rock Valley Road) becomes impassable when wet, and the slot floor can hold water that's ankle-deep and cold. Beautiful but miserable.
- **Any day with rain in the upstream forecast within 24 hours.** No exceptions.

The right windows are March-through-May (right now) and September-through-November. Within those, the right days are clear-sky days with no monsoon developing.
