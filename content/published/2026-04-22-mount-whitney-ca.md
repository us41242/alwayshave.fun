## Mount Whitney: The Full Guide

Mount Whitney at 14,505 feet is the highest peak in the contiguous United States. The Main Trail from Whitney Portal is 22 miles out-and-back with 6,100 feet of elevation gain. The trail climbs through five distinct zones: the forested lower canyon, the granite cascade near Lone Pine Lake, the exposed switchbacks above Trail Camp, the 99 Switchbacks to Trail Crest at 13,600 feet, and the final 2-mile ridge traverse to the summit.

Every step of that is worth it. The summit views on a clear day stretch from Nevada to the Pacific. I've been up there twice and both times I stood at the register box at the top and thought: this is why I do this.

**Difficulty:** Expert. Not because any individual section is technically difficult — there's no climbing, no roped exposure — but because the combination of distance, altitude, elevation gain, and variable weather creates a serious objective hazard. Altitude sickness is common even in fit hikers. The turnaround decision at Trail Camp (where most people who don't summit turn back) requires honest self-assessment.

**Length:** 22 miles, 6,100 ft gain. Allow 12–16 hours for a day attempt. Most people do it as an overnight with a camp at either Outpost Camp (mile 3.5) or Trail Camp (mile 6, at 12,000 feet).

## The Dog-Friendly Situation

This trail runs through Inyo National Forest, which allows leashed dogs. The trail does not pass through any section of Sequoia National Park where dogs would be restricted to paved surfaces.

**The catch:** Dogs at this elevation and distance require their own preparation. The 6,100 feet of gain over 22 miles on rocky, exposed terrain is hard on paws. Kipper has done long alpine days with me, but I bring her on overnight mountain missions more cautiously than I used to after watching her favor one foot coming down from a descent with a lot of talus.

If your dog is used to long, technical days, carry their food and water (water is available from streams in the lower canyon but gets scarce higher up), bring booties if the upper trail has ice, and watch their breathing at altitude. Dogs can get altitude sickness too.

For a summer summit attempt with your dog, I'd recommend testing them on the first 6 miles to Lone Pine Lake and back as a separate day before committing to the full 22.

## Permit Information

**May through November:** A permit is required for day hiking the Mount Whitney Zone. The permit lottery opens in February for the full season. Quota is 100 day hikers per day.

**Right now (April):** No permit required. April is the shoulder window between the winter closure and the permit season. You can attempt Whitney in April without a lottery win — which is actually the appeal for some experienced hikers who want the route without the competition for permits.

The tradeoff: April conditions. See above.

**How to get a permit for summer:** recreation.gov. The lottery fills fast. If you don't win the main lottery, check the system daily in May and June for cancellation releases. People cancel permits constantly.

## Gear for a Whitney Attempt

This is the most gear-intensive trail I cover on alwayshave.fun. The conditions today (April) add an extra layer on top of the standard summer kit.

**For today (April, extreme conditions):**
- Mountaineering boots or insulated approach shoes with crampon compatibility
- Microspikes at minimum; crampons recommended above Trail Crest
- Ice axe if you have the training
- Expedition weight insulation: down jacket, down pants, OR a puffy plus waterproof shell
- Wind layers are not enough — the -20°F wind chill requires insulation
- Balaclava, ski goggles, heavyweight gloves + overmitts
- Poles — mandatory for the descent

**For summer (July–October, more typical conditions):**
- Lightweight trail runners or approach shoes
- Layers for summit (weather can change in 20 minutes at 14,000 feet)
- Sun protection is serious — UV index 9 today, typically higher in summer at this altitude
- 4–5 liters water capacity; filter for streams in the lower canyon
- Headlamp — most summiteers start at 1–3am to beat afternoon thunderstorms

**Always:** satellite communicator. Cell service disappears by Trail Camp. If something goes wrong above Trail Crest, search and rescue needs to know where you are.

## Parking and Access

Whitney Portal Road leads to the trailhead at 8,360 feet. The road is currently open (check CalTrans for current road conditions before driving up — it can close with spring storms). Whitney Portal has a small store, pay parking, and vault toilets.

From Lone Pine, it's 13 miles on Whitney Portal Road. The drive alone gains 4,700 feet. If you're coming from sea level, don't skip the acclimatization step — a night in Lone Pine (3,700 ft) or Bishop (4,150 ft) before your summit attempt materially reduces altitude sickness risk.

## The Bottom Line

Whitney is one of the most rewarding trails I've ever been on, and I'll be going back. But not in April. Not in these conditions.

Watch the forecast, get your permit sorted for July, and if you want to experience the lower canyon without the summit commitment, the hike to Lone Pine Lake (6 miles, 2,900 ft gain) is dog-friendly, gorgeous, and reasonable in April with the right gear.

The summit will be there when the conditions are right. That's kind of the whole point.
