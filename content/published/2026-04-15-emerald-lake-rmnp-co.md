Here is what Emerald Lake looks like today: a hole in a snowfield with some pine trees around it.

That's not a criticism. It's April at 10,110 feet. Dreams and Nymph and Emerald are still under the kind of ice that looks solid right up until it isn't, and the trail between them is a packed track of boot-prints and spindrift that refreezes every night. The score system is calling it 60 out of 100 and flagging **wind layer recommended** and **insulation layer required**, and the score is being generous because the four-day forecast is about to get worse before it gets better.

Kelly and Mark are not coming on this one. They are never coming on this one. No dogs in RMNP outside of campgrounds and paved roads, which is a rule I grumble about and also secretly respect.

## What "Easy, 3.6 Miles" Means Right Now

The Emerald Lake chain — Nymph Lake, Dream Lake, Emerald Lake — is a summer beginner trail. Families do it. Grandparents do it. In late June it's a 3.6-mile round trip with 655 feet of gain and the only thing stopping you is the timed-entry permit.

In April, it is a **winter hike**. The trail is packed snow from Bear Lake trailhead all the way up. Microspikes are mandatory. Snowshoes are better on the north-facing stretches between Dream and Emerald, which don't see direct sun until late morning and hold powder deeper than you'd expect.

If you have never winter-hiked before, this is not the day to learn. If you have winter-hiked before, you already knew all of this and you already have your gear in the trunk.

## The Timed Entry Question

Here's the one thing I actually want you to leave this article knowing:

**RMNP timed entry for the Bear Lake Road corridor starts in late May.** It runs roughly Memorial Day weekend through mid-October, same as the last several seasons. Right now, in April, there is **no timed entry**. The road is open. The parking is free. Bear Lake lot is the emptiest it will be all year.

This is, counterintuitively, why April is actually a nice time to be up there — if you're treating it like what it is, which is a winter snowshoe, not a summer hike.

When timed entry opens, it gets announced on the NPS Rocky Mountain National Park page in the weeks leading up. The permits release on **recreation.gov** on a rolling basis — some released months out, some in a two-day window. If you're planning a summer trip, set a calendar alarm the first week of May to check the release schedule. The Bear Lake permits sell out fastest.

## Trail Reality, If You're Going Anyway

If you're committed to Emerald Lake this week, here's the honest play:

1. **Go tomorrow (Thursday)** or **Friday.** Saturday and Sunday are storm days.
2. **Start at Bear Lake trailhead at sunrise** — 6:22 AM tomorrow. The snow is firm, the wind is usually lighter, and you're off the exposed sections before afternoon.
3. **Microspikes from the parking lot.** Not optional. The first 200 yards from the Bear Lake trailhead is glazed from foot traffic.
4. **Turn around at Dream Lake** if the wind above the trees is worse than forecast. Dream is the pretty one anyway — the cirque walls of Hallett Peak come up hard behind the lake and on a clear winter day it's one of the best short-hike views in Colorado.
5. **Bring real insulation.** The gear flag is not decorative. A down or synthetic puffy, wind shell, gloves that actually work, and a beanie. If you're a sweater, a second base layer in the pack for the descent.

## What To Do Instead

You drove to Estes Park and the trail is a winter hike. Fine. Options:

- **Bear Lake itself** — flat, 0.8-mile loop around the lake. Gorgeous in April, microspikes plenty, no elevation to speak of.
- **Gem Lake** (Lumpy Ridge) — lower elevation, more likely to be dry-ish tread this time of year, and a solid 3.2-mile round trip with a pretty little lake at the top.
- **Lily Lake** — 0.8 miles, paved, year-round. For recovery days, date days, or days when the weather wins.
- **Drive west to Grand Lake side** — typically even snowier, but the Cascade Falls trail from North Inlet is a nice snowshoe.
- **Go south instead** — Garden of the Gods is scoring in the 90s right now and Kelly can do that one without looking tragic.

## The Dog Question

Zero dogs in RMNP on any trail, period. Not on Emerald Lake, not on Bear Lake, not on Gem Lake. If you've been relying on the shoulder season to squeeze your dog in — the rule still applies. Kelly and Mark stayed home with my partner for this one; Estes Park has a few daycare options if you're in town for a few days and didn't plan ahead.

This is actually one of the clearer dog-friendly calls in Colorado: drop down to **Roosevelt National Forest** right next door (Lion Gulch, Twin Sisters — Twin Sisters requires an RMNP permit technically but Lion Gulch doesn't), or detour south to **Mount Evans area / Arapaho** where dogs are fine on most forest trails.

## John's Take

The photo you want of Emerald Lake — black water, the cirque of Hallett and Flattop behind it, green-tinged in morning light, maybe a loon on the surface — is a **mid-July to early-September** photo. Any earlier and the lake is frozen or just thawing and the experience is fundamentally different. Any later and you're racing sunset in thinning air.

The play is this: use this week to check on the **Bear Lake Road** status and get your summer timed-entry calendar set. Then come back in late June through August, stay in Estes for two nights, do Emerald Lake on a 6 AM permit slot, do Sky Pond the next day, and eat a weirdly good pretzel at the brewery downtown afterward.

Kelly and Mark will still be at home for that trip too. That's the rule up here. But there's a park bench on the Estes Park riverwalk where a Shih Tzu can sit with a pup cup while the humans talk about the trail, and that's basically the whole RMNP dog experience in a sentence.
