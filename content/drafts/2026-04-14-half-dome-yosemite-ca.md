Here is the situation at Half Dome today: 30°F at the trailhead overnight, a high of 34°F this afternoon, 42% chance of rain still hanging in the forecast from yesterday's system. The score is 60 out of 100. It's not a bad day, but it's not the day.

Now look at tomorrow.

Wednesday, April 15th: high of 42°F, low of 24°F, 1% chance of rain, AQI 36 — clean and clear. The score jumps to 88 out of 100, which is the system's "Great day to go" tier. After Wednesday the week gets variable again: 70 on Thursday, 88 on Friday. You have actual windows here, and you should use one of them.

There is one thing I want to be clear about before you start packing: **the cables aren't up yet.** Half Dome's cable route — the final 400-foot haul up the exposed granite slabs — runs from roughly Memorial Day through mid-October. In April, the upper dome is still carrying ice and snow above 8,800 feet. The NPS doesn't install the cables in spring conditions, and they shouldn't. So the Half Dome you're hiking this week is not the summit Half Dome.

That said: the approach to Half Dome sub-dome is one of the best wilderness hikes in California. It's 14.2 miles out and back, 4,800 feet of gain, and it takes you through Little Yosemite Valley, past Nevada Fall roaring at peak snowmelt, up the John Muir Trail through some of the quietest granite corridor you'll experience in Yosemite. You come out at the base of the sub-dome with the cables coiled up and the granite slabs rising above you. It is not a consolation prize.

## The April Version of This Hike

The approach from Happy Isles trailhead follows the Mist Trail or the John Muir Trail up to Nevada Fall, then the JMT continuation through Little Yosemite Valley to the Half Dome shoulder. The first two miles climb through the spray zone above Vernal Fall — in April the Merced is carrying a full season of Sierra snowmelt and you will feel it on the staircase. Waterproof jacket is not optional on that section regardless of the forecast.

Above Nevada Fall the trail settles into a long, moderate climb through Little Yosemite Valley. This is where April Yosemite earns its reputation — fewer than 10% of summer crowds, the campground mostly quiet, the valley floor still in early spring green. It's spectacular in a different way than the summer version.

Above 7,500 feet, expect shaded north-facing sections with ice. Microspikes in your pack are not overkill — they're the difference between confidently covering ground and white-knuckling a 30-foot traverse on refrozen snow. You won't need them the whole way, but you'll know when you need them.

At the sub-dome base, you'll see the cable posts still bare. That's your turnaround. Budget 5–6 hours for the full approach and return, more if conditions are slower than expected.

## Permit Logistics (For When the Cables Are Actually Up)

The cable permit is a lottery. The pre-season lottery for 2026 ran in March — if you missed it, your path is the **daily lottery**: 50 permits released at 4pm, two days before the date you want to hike. Competitive, but people get them.

No permit is needed right now for the sub-dome approach in April. Once cables go up (typically mid to late May, confirmed via the NPS Yosemite site and social channels a week or two before installation), the permit requirement kicks in immediately.

For summer planning: set a calendar reminder now for the first day daily permits open, which is usually Memorial Day weekend. They release two days out, so Thursday afternoon permits the Saturday slot.

## The Shuttle Situation

The Happy Isles trailhead is accessed by Yosemite Valley shuttle from Curry Village. In April, the first bus typically runs around 7am — check the NPS site or the visitor center day-of, since spring maintenance schedules can shift things. Parking in the valley is possible early but fills fast on weekends even in shoulder season.

If you're staying at Yosemite Valley Lodge or nearby, the shuttle stops are clearly marked. If you're driving in from outside, aim for the main Valley parking lots by 7am and take the shuttle to Happy Isles from there.

## Kipper Stays Home

No dogs on any trails in Yosemite, including the full Half Dome corridor from Happy Isles up. That includes the JMT approach — not just the summit push. Kipper stayed with my neighbor this week; I have a running arrangement where I watch her beagle one weekend a month and she takes Kipper when I disappear into the park. If you don't have a setup like that, Valley kennels and Mariposa boarding book out fast for spring weekends. Start those calls now if you're planning ahead.
