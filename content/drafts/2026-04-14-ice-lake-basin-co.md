Kelly looked at the forecast over my shoulder this morning and left the room. Mark sat down in the kitchen and didn't get up for a while. I respect both of their assessments.

It is the mountain's polite way of telling you to go home, wait two months, and come back when it's actually ready.

## What The Trail Looks Like Right Now

Ice Lake Basin tops out above 12,500 feet. The 7-mile out-and-back with 2,600 feet of gain is a genuine workout in ideal conditions. In April, the trail above the lower meadow is covered in snow that has been frozen and refrozen for weeks. You're post-holing through sections where the path is invisible. The lake at the end — the turquoise one from all the photographs — is under a solid foot of ice and another foot of snow on top of that.

The photographs you've been saving were taken in July. The wildflower explosion that makes this basin one of the most photographed places in Colorado happens in late July into early August. What's up there right now is a very different experience, and it requires crampons, avalanche awareness, navigation skills in a snow-covered landscape, and the realistic understanding that conditions can deteriorate faster at this elevation than you'd expect.

High-clearance vehicle is required to reach the South Mineral Campground trailhead. In April, that road may have its own opinion about whether you're arriving at all.

## The Dog Question

Ice Lake Basin is dog-friendly on-leash, which it gets points for. Kelly and Mark are therefore technically eligible to participate. Practically, at 28°F with 18 mph winds and 75% precipitation odds, I would be carrying two eight-pound Shih Tzus in my pack before we hit mile 2 while they gave me the specific look they reserve for situations that are my fault. Their legs are not long enough to clear post-hole depth. Their paws are not equipped for a sustained icy traverse. They would be cold, they would be done, and the whole expedition would become a rescue operation for two dogs who did not agree to this.

I'll bring them in July. The San Juan wildflower meadows in late summer are genuinely good for small dogs — the trail is walkable, the temperatures are reasonable, and Kelly will pretend she doesn't like it while obviously liking it. Mark will openly enjoy himself for about four minutes before sitting down and needing to be coaxed back up. This is the full range of Shih Tzu emotional expression on a mountain, and I am used to it.

## When It Actually Opens

Best months for Ice Lake Basin are July through September. In a normal snow year, the trail is generally snow-free by late June at lower elevations and late July in the basin itself. After a heavy winter — which this has been — push those estimates by two to three weeks.

The wildflowers peak late July into early August. That's the trip. If you want the version of Ice Lake Basin that looks like the photos, you want the last week of July or the first week of August. Not April.

Silverton is the base for this hike and it books up fast in summer. Accommodations are limited and the town is not big. Start looking at lodging now if you're planning a July trip — not in June when everything is already gone.

## What To Do This Week Instead

If you're in Southwest Colorado and the energy is still there:

- **Mesa Verde National Park** — lower elevation, year-round accessible, and genuinely interesting in a different way than a mountain hike. The ancestral Puebloan sites are harder to appreciate in summer crowds.
- **Colorado National Monument (Grand Junction)** — Western Slope desert, current temps in the 50s and 60s, red rock canyon hiking with no altitude issues.
- **Ridgway State Park** — reservoir views, dog-friendly, Front Range-adjacent, no crampons required.
- **Ouray Ice Park** — if you're already near Silverton, the ice climbing season typically runs through mid-April. You won't hike Ice Lake Basin, but you'll be in the same dramatic canyon and you'll watch people do things that are objectively impressive.

The consistent thread: stay below 8,000 feet this week and Colorado is a perfectly good place to be. Above 10,000 feet and the calendar is lying to you.

## John's Take

Ice Lake Basin in July is one of the better hikes in Colorado. The basin is genuine — not Instagram-manufactured, not crowded relative to the scenery — and the wildflower display is the real thing. It is worth the drive to Silverton and the high-clearance vehicle requirement and the 7am start to beat afternoon thunderstorms.

It is not worth attempting in April. The score is 60 all week. The high on Friday is 22°F. The mountain is still in winter and it does not care that the calendar says spring.

Wait for July. Book the lodging before you need to. Set an alarm for the day you want to go and plan to leave before 7am. Bring the dogs if they're cleared for mountain trails — or leave them home with someone reliable if they're not the kind of dogs who were built for this, which mine definitively are not.

The window opens. It always does up here. Just not yet.
